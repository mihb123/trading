# Multi-Timeframe Analysis: The Complete Forex Guide (2026)

Multi-timeframe analysis (MTF) fixes this. The golden rule is brutally simple: trade in the direction of the higher timeframe, and use the lower timeframe only to refine the entry. This is the top-down framework that institutional traders have used for decades and that separates professionals from retail — not the patterns they read, but the sequence in which they read them. This guide walks through the complete MTF playbook: the four-chart cascade from W1 down to M15, the two-timeframe rule for busy traders, the stacked-entry templates that actually work, and the critical question of what to do when your higher and lower timeframes disagree.

## Key Takeaways
- The golden rule: trade in the direction of the higher timeframe, use the lower timeframe only to refine the entry.
- Most traders only need two timeframes — one for bias, one for execution — not four.
- The standard cascade is W1 → D1 → H4 → H1 → M15. Pick the two adjacent charts that fit your style.
- HTF resistance plus LTF rejection, and HTF support plus LTF engulfing, are two of the highest-edge setups in forex.
- When higher and lower timeframes disagree, the HTF wins — or you stay out.

## 1. Weekly to Daily — Macro Bias
The first question is not 'is this a good setup' but 'what is the macro trend?' Answer on the weekly chart. W1 tells you whether a pair has been grinding higher for six months, rolling over, or locked in a sideways range. Nothing on H1 or M15 overrides that context.

Weekly-to-daily cascade: open W1, identify dominant trend, mark significant structural levels, drop to D1 and look for recent behaviour that confirms or challenges the weekly read. A pair making higher weekly highs should be making higher daily lows; if the daily is printing lower highs while weekly still looks bullish, the macro move is losing momentum.

## 2. Daily to H4 — Narrowing the Setup
The daily-to-H4 pairing is the working-trader's default. One D1 candle equals six H4 candles. Daily defines bias; H4 defines setup. Most profitable swing traders operate exclusively in this zone.

Workflow: open D1, confirm trend direction, identify most relevant swing high/low, drop to H4 and look for a pullback into that level. Classic H4 entry triggers: engulfing bars at prior resistance-turned-support, pin bars off the 20-period EMA, and break-and-retest formations.

## 3. H4 to H1 — Refining the Entry Zone
H4-to-H1 is where active day traders live. Four H1 candles per H4 bar. Process: on H4, identify direction and mark recent demand/supply zone. On H1, wait for price to reach that zone and produce a confirmation signal (pin bar, engulfing, failed-auction reversal). Stop typically on the far side of the H4 swing (40-80 pips).

## 4. H1 to M15 — Precision Execution
Short-horizon scalper's version. H1 sets intraday bias, M15 gives resolution to time entry. Ratio: four M15 per H1 bar. Stops 15-30 pips, targets at next H1 swing. Best for London-open and NY-open breakout systems.

## 5. The Top-Down Analysis Framework (4-Step Flow)
1. Macro bias — Weekly/Daily: Open W1, identify trend/range/reversal. Drop to D1. Write one word: bullish, bearish, or neutral.
2. Setup zone — Daily/H4: On D1/H4, locate specific supply/demand zone where bias-direction trade exists.
3. Trigger — H1/M15: Drop to H1/M15. Wait for price to arrive at zone. Require confirmation candle.
4. Risk — HTF-defined: Place stop on far side of HTF zone, not LTF candle. Size position to risk fixed percentage. Target next HTF structural level.

## 6. HTF Resistance + LTF Rejection Entry
Higher-timeframe resistance (daily or H4 swing high tested at least once) sets up a short. Price rallies back; on LTF wait for clear rejection candle. Short, stop above HTF high, target most recent swing low. Win rates 55-65% with R-multiples of 2-3 on winners.

## 7. HTF Support + LTF Engulfing Entry
Bullish mirror. Daily or H4 support sets up a long. Price pulls back; on H1/M15 wait for bullish engulfing. Engulfing matters more than pin bar here — requires genuine two-sided flow. Filter: check HTF support sits in uptrend context.

## 8. HTF Trend + LTF Pullback Entry
Pure trend-following MTF entry. On D1/H4, clear directional move. Do NOT trade initial breakout; wait for pullback on LTF. When pullback produces higher low on LTF and first bullish candle closes above prior bar's high, enter. Key: patience during pullback.

## 9. HTF Range + LTF Reversal at Extreme
Most forex pairs spend majority of time in ranges. Identify HTF range boundaries, wait for price to reach one extreme, use LTF to time reversal back to middle. Critical filter: ensure range is intact (no recent HH/HL breaking out).

## 10. Conflict Resolution — HTF vs LTF
Rule: the higher timeframe wins. Always. If D1 is bullish and H1 is bearish, the H1 bearish move is a pullback, not a reversal. Do not short it. Either wait for pullback to complete and re-enter long, or stay out. When conflict persists for more than a day or two, the HTF is in the process of shifting — flatten exposure.
